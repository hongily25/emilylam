Daily Questions #3

January 14, 2015

1. What is Broca's aphasia? What does the speech of a Broca's aphasic sound like?

Broca's aphasia is a type of speech impairment located in the left frontal lobe. It sounds like broken speech. People with Broca's aphasia have difficulty with syntax. They speak slowly and have lots of pauses.

2. What is Wernicke's aphasia? What does the speech of a Wernicke's aphasic sound like?

Wernicke's aphasia is a type of speech impairment located in the posterior temporal gyrus. The speech of a Wernicke's aphasic does not make semantic sense but the syntax is OK. There is some phonological impairment.

3. What characterizes a split brain patient? What does it tell us about how the brain organizes language functions?

A split brain patient has their corpus callosum severed so their right hemisphere cannot communicate with their left hemisphere. It tells us that the left side of the brain is responsive for speech. The right side may not be able to speak but it can still draw shapes. It tells us that the brain has independent functions.

4. Name the four types of non-invasive measurements that we discussed in class. Which measure blood flow and which measure electromagnetic field change?

Hemodynamic
  1. fMRI - compares oxygen levels in different parts of the brain: the more active an area is, the more oxygenated blood it needs.
  2. PET - Introduces a radioactive tracer into the blood stream and measures gamma rays during positron emission decay.

    3. Electromagnetic

    1. EEG - Measures the electrical field around scalp. changes in large gorups of neurons result in spike in electrical activity.
    2. MEG - measures the magnetic field around scalp associated with same types of events as EEG.

5. What linguistic elements are the P600 and N400 components in an ERP waveform sensitive to? Write which effect we would expect to see (including neither and both) in the examples below, and explain why. a. The cup broked on the floor. b. My dog is good at math. c. Shirley drove to the store last week.

P400 is sensitive to semantic anomalies. P600 is sensitive to syntactic anomalies.

a. The cup broked on the floor. P600
b. My dog is good at math. P400
c. Shirley drove to the store last week. Neither.

Daily Questions #2

January 7, 2015

1. What is fast mapping? How do we know whether it is unique to human language?

Fast mapping is associating the meaning of a word with its referent in a single exposure. It is single trial learning. It is not unique to human language. Animals like dogs have mechanism for intelligence that we also share. A border collie can recognize new stuffed animals by name through the process of elimination.

2. What are some of the limitations on complex language ability observed from nonhuman primate studies?

Children learn without explicit instruction while dogs require extensive training. Dogs also don't have vocal chords. For chimps, they created few spontaneous signs. The word order was idosyncratic.

3. What is the critical period? What evidence do we have for it? How does it support the idea
that language is a biological system?

A critical period is the developmental time period in which language is learned. It is also a period of neural plasticity. The evidence for it is that babies who speak another language before puberty can become bilingual. A girl named Genie was abused and didn't learn language during the critical period. As a result, she never was able to speak properly. She couldn't use auxiliaries for example. This supports the idea that language is a biological system because Genie could not be trained to speak English properly. It shows that language must be learned during the critical period or else it is lost forever.

4. What is a homesign system? Explain how Nicaraguan Sign Language is different.

A homesign system is a set of gestural signs spontaneously developed by deaf individuals to communicate when sign language is unknown. Nicaraguan Sign Language was developed by deaf children with considerable complexity. Younger students were able to adopt and learn twice as fast.

5. Explain the concept of double dissociation. How is it used in language research to support
the idea that language is domain specific?

Double association is the showing independence by finding distinct situations in which there's cognitive but not language impairment and language but not cognitive impairment. It supports the idea that language is domain specific, meaning some linguistic functions develop as modules.

Daily Questions #1

January 5, 2015

1. Describe in your own words, what a model is. Give an example.

A model is a representation of reality. It should be guided by a research question and explain a desired phenomena. For example, given an image of a cat, a model can be a perfect fit, a good fit, or a poor fit. A perfect fit is an exact image of the cat. It is not simple nor generalizable. A good fit is a drawing of a cat. It is not the same as the image, but the drawing captures the qualities of the cat well. A poor fit is a cat stick figure. It is too simple and does not capture the likeless of the cat well.

2. What are the two principles of model building?

Model building should be predictive and falsifiable. It should also be simple but still capture the data accurately.

3. What is the difference between a sample and a population? Which do we tend to test in experiments and why?

A population is the group one's study is about. A sample is the group that one's study actually tested. We usually test a sample because a population is too large.

4. What is meant by a module? Give one property and explain its relation to illusions.

A module is a discrete processing unit that is organized by component parts. It is fast, automatic, mandatory, and isolated from other modules. The two pictures of Maragaret Thatcher is an optical illusion. We look for eyes and mouths to be oriented a certain way. When a face is upside but the eyes and mouths remain in their original positions, the image looks acceptable. But when we turn the image right-side up, the image looks completely distorted.
